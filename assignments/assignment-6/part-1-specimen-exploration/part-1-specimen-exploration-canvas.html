Type Explorer & Font Specialist

Part 1: Typeface Specimen & Exploration

Project Context

In this part, you become a Type Explorer. You will choose one typeface and explore it exhaustively through a specimen poster that showcases its personality, capabilities, and technical details. This is where you develop true typography mastery.

The Brief

Select one typeface and create a comprehensive specimen poster that explores its characteristics, demonstrates proper spacing (kerning, leading, tracking), and showcases its personality across different sizes and weights.

Core Learning Goals

  • See letterforms clearly: Understand type anatomy and design details
  • Master spacing: Learn the difference between kerning, leading, and tracking
  • Understand weight and style: Explore bold, italic, light, and other variations
  • Build proficiency: Learn how to kern manually and space text professionally
  • Develop type literacy: Know when and how to use each typeface effectively

Understanding Your Typeface

Classification

  • Is it serif, sans-serif, display, script, or monospace?
  • When was it designed?
  • What is its intended use? (Headline, body copy, web, print?)

Psychology & Personality

  • What emotion does it convey? (Bold, elegant, playful, serious, technical?)
  • What brands or contexts use it? Why?
  • What's its "voice"? How would it sound if it talked?

Type Anatomy

  • What is its x-height? (Large x-height = readable at small sizes)
  • Are the serifs (if any) thick or thin?
  • What is the stroke weight variation?
  • Are the letterforms open or closed?
  • Notable design quirks?

Technical Details

  • How many weights does it have?
  • Does it have italics?
  • How well does it kern by default?
  • Is it web-safe or do you need a custom font file?

The Specimen Poster Structure

Section % of Poster What to Include
1. Title & Information 15% Typeface name, designer, year, classification
2. Pangram Exploration 40% Full pangram at 3-5 sizes, 2+ weights
3. Alphabet & Numerals 30% Full alphabet, 0-9, punctuation marks
4. Anatomy Callouts 10% Baseline, cap height, x-height, serifs, counters
5. Spacing Demo 5% Before/after kerning, leading variations

🎯 The Kerning Challenge

Kerning is NOT equal spacing. It's perceived equal spacing based on the eye's interpretation of letter shapes.

Letters That Need Kerning:

AV, AW, AT, AC, Ty, To, Te, We, Wo, Wa, Ye, Yo, Ya, LT, LA, LY, VA, VE, VO, PA, PE, PO

Your kerning demonstration: Show one problematic pair with default kerning → after manual kerning adjustment.

Design Process (10-15 hours total)

Step 1: Choose Your Typeface (1 hour)

Select ONE typeface with at least 2 weights. Good choices: Georgia, Montserrat, Playfair Display, Dancing Script.

Step 2: Analyze (1-2 hours)

Study typeface deeply. Answer all analysis questions. Identify kerning problem pairs. Plan layout on paper.

Step 3: Design Layout (2-3 hours)

Sketch rough layout. Decide hierarchy. Plan white space. Create grid structure. Determine sizing.

Step 4: Digital Execution (3-5 hours)

Set up 11" × 17" document at 300 DPI. Place elements. Manually kern problem pairs. Add anatomy callouts. Check spacing.

Step 5: Refinement & Polish (2-3 hours)

Review for legibility. Ensure white space is generous. Verify kerning is intentional. Export at 300 DPI.

Technical Specifications

  • Dimensions: 11" × 17" (portrait) or 8.5" × 11"
  • Resolution: 300 DPI
  • Color Mode: CMYK (printing) or RGB (digital)
  • File Format: PDF, PNG, or high-res image
  • Tools: Illustrator, InDesign, Figma, or Affinity Designer

💡 Teacher's Tips

The "Zoom Test"

Zoom in 200% on different sections. Can you still read clearly? Is white space balanced? Does hierarchy guide the eye?

The "Squint Test"

Squint while looking. Does the title stand out? Can you still identify sections? Does it look balanced or cramped?

The "Hang Test"

If printing, hang it on a wall. View from across the room. Does it communicate the typeface's personality? Is it legible from distance?

Submission Format

  1. Digital Specimen Poster: High-resolution PDF or PNG (300 DPI, 11" × 17")
  2. Source File: The original design file (.ai, .indd, .fig, or .afdesign)
  3. Typeface Analysis Document (300-400 words):
    • What typeface did you choose and why?
    • Describe its personality and intended use
    • What did you learn about its design details?
    • How did you demonstrate kerning, leading, and tracking?
    • What was the most challenging aspect?
    • How would you use this typeface in a real project?
