## Part 2: Typographic Hierarchy & Communication

### The Brief
Design a **layout-based project** (event poster, article cover, brochure cover, book jacket, or editorial spread) that uses **multiple typefaces** to establish clear **visual hierarchy** and guide the reader through the information in a logical, compelling way.

Your challenge: **Use typography alone** (no images, minimal color) **to organize and communicate information effectively**.

### Core Learning Goals
* **Information Hierarchy:** Distinguish importance levels through typographic contrast (size, weight, color, spacing)
* **Typeface Pairing:** Choose complementary fonts that work together (not against each other)
* **Visual Flow:** Guide the reader's eye through information in an intentional sequence
* **White Space Mastery:** Use space strategically to organize information and rest the eye
* **Typographic Expression:** Let font choices communicate mood and personality
* **Readability at Scale:** Balance aesthetics with comfort and legibility

## Understanding Visual Hierarchy

### The Hierarchy Pyramid

```
Level 1: PRIMARY (Most Important)
↓ Largest, boldest, most prominent
↓ What should the viewer see FIRST?

Level 2: SECONDARY (Important Supporting Info)
↓ Medium size, supporting role
↓ What explains or clarifies the primary?

Level 3: TERTIARY (Details & Context)
↓ Smallest, least visually prominent
↓ The details that flesh out understanding

Level 4: QUATERNARY (Fine Print / Legal)
↓ Smallest, often footnotes or disclaimers
```

### The 70-20-10 Rule (Applied to Type)

In any layout, approximately:
- **70% of visual attention** goes to the primary element (headline, title, most important info)
- **20% of visual attention** goes to secondary elements (subheading, supporting information)
- **10% of visual attention** goes to tertiary elements (details, fine print)

### Five Techniques for Creating Hierarchy (Without Color)

**1. SIZE**
- Primary text is 3-5x larger than body text
- Clear size jumps between levels (don't use subtle differences)
- Example: 72pt headline → 36pt subheading → 14pt body

**2. WEIGHT**
- Primary uses bold or extra-bold (700-900)
- Secondary uses semi-bold or bold (600-700)
- Tertiary uses regular or light (400-500)
- Creates contrast without changing typeface

**3. SCALE & PROPORTION**
- Dramatic size difference = dramatic importance
- Subtle size difference = subtle importance
- Space around an element defines its importance (isolation = emphasis)

**4. ALIGNMENT & PLACEMENT**
- Center alignment = formal, important
- Left alignment = active, dynamic, default for body copy
- Strategic placement at top/center/bottom affects importance

**5. SPACING & DENSITY**
- Tight spacing = compressed, urgent, active
- Generous spacing = open, refined, important
- Use white space to "frame" important information

## Typeface Pairing Guide

Choosing typefaces that work together is a critical skill.

### Successful Pairing Strategies

**Strategy 1: Serif + Sans-Serif (The Classic Pairing)**
- Primary: Serif typeface for headlines (traditional, authoritative)
- Secondary: Complementary sans-serif for body (modern, clean)
- Example: Playfair Display (serif headline) + Open Sans (sans-serif body)

**Strategy 2: Contrasting Sans-Serif Styles**
- Primary: Display sans-serif (geometric, bold) for headlines
- Secondary: Humanist sans-serif (friendly, open) for body
- Example: Montserrat Bold (geometric) + Lato (humanist)

**Strategy 3: Heavy Display + Light Supporting Font**
- Primary: Bold, expressive display font for headlines
- Secondary: Refined, elegant font for supporting information
- Example: Bebas Neue (heavy) + Garamond (refined)

**Strategy 4: Same Family, Different Weights/Styles**
- Primary: Bold or italic version of font family
- Secondary: Regular or light version of same family
- Example: Open Sans Bold (headline) + Open Sans Regular (body)

### Pairing Don'ts
- ❌ Don't pair two serif fonts (unless intentional, can feel dated)
- ❌ Don't pair two display fonts (visual chaos)
- ❌ Don't pair fonts too similar (confusing, no contrast)
- ❌ Don't pair fonts too different (visual discord, no harmony)
- ❌ Don't use more than 3 typefaces (loss of coherence)

### Recommended Pairings
* **Elegant + Professional:** Garamond + Montserrat
* **Modern + Approachable:** Lato + Playfair Display
* **Bold + Contemporary:** Bebas Neue + Open Sans
* **Classic + Clean:** Georgia + Helvetica
* **Luxury + Refined:** Bodoni + Lato

## The Design Process

### Step 1: Content Selection (1-2 hours)
* Choose your project type
* Gather real content (or create compelling fictional content)
* Understand the information hierarchy
  * What's most important for the reader to know first?
  * What's supporting information?
  * What's fine print or context?

### Step 2: Typeface Research & Selection (1-2 hours)
* Research 5-10 typeface pairings
* Make mood boards showing different pairing options
* Decide on your primary and secondary fonts
* Test them together at actual sizes

### Step 3: Layout Sketching (2-3 hours)
* Sketch 5+ layout directions by hand
* Experiment with different information arrangements
* Test hierarchy techniques: size, weight, alignment, spacing
* Don't get attached to the first idea—explore multiple directions

### Step 4: Digital Mockup (4-6 hours)
* Create document at proper size/resolution
* Lay out information in your chosen fonts
* Apply hierarchy principles:
  * Does the most important info stand out?
  * Can a reader understand the information flow in 5 seconds?
  * Is white space working strategically?
* Adjust sizes, weights, spacing until it feels right

### Step 5: Refinement & Testing (2-3 hours)
* Print or view on large screen
* Ask: Does the hierarchy guide my eye naturally?
* Check: Is all text legible at intended size?
* Verify: Do the typefaces complement each other?
* Polish: Is the white space balanced throughout?

## Technical Requirements

### File Specifications
* **Dimensions:**
  * Event Poster: 18" × 24" or 11" × 17"
  * Magazine Cover: 8.5" × 11" or 8.375" × 10.875" (standard magazine)
  * Book Jacket: 6" × 9" (standard paperback) or custom
  * Brochure/Spread: 8.5" × 11" (single spread) or 11" × 8.5" (open spread)

* **Resolution:** 300 DPI (for print quality)
* **Color Mode:** CMYK (printing) or RGB (digital)
* **File Format:** PDF (preferred), PNG, or TIFF

### Design Principles
* **Hierarchy:** Information organized by clear size/weight/placement contrasts
* **White Space:** Generous negative space organizing information
* **Legibility:** All text easily readable at intended viewing distance
* **Cohesion:** Typeface pairing feels intentional and harmonious
* **Simplicity:** Focus on typography—no images or unnecessary color

### What NOT to Do
* ❌ Don't rely on color for hierarchy (use typography only)
* ❌ Don't add images that distract from typography
* ❌ Don't use more than 3 typefaces
* ❌ Don't ignore white space—it's a design element
* ❌ Don't use typography that doesn't match the content's personality

## 💡 Teacher's Tips

### The "Five-Second Test"
Show your layout for 5 seconds, then ask:
- Can someone tell you what's most important?
- Can they identify the primary message?
- Do they understand roughly what the piece is about?

If not, strengthen your hierarchy.

### The "Squint Test"
Squint while looking at your layout:
- Does the primary information still stand out?
- Can you still trace a visual path through the page?
- Is white space balanced?

### The "Print Test"
Print your layout at full size (if possible):
- Is all text legible at intended viewing distance?
- Do the typefaces look good together in person?
- Is the spacing working as intended?
