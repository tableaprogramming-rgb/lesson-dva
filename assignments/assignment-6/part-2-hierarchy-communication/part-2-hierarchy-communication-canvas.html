Information Architect & Typographic Designer

Part 2: Typographic Hierarchy & Communication

Project Context

Now that you've mastered a single typeface deeply, you're ready for the real challenge: using typography to organize and communicate. You'll select real-world content and organize it using multiple typefaces and sophisticated hierarchy. Typography is no longer about beauty—it's about clarity, flow, and guiding readers through information.

The Brief

Design a layout-based project (event poster, article cover, brochure, or book jacket) that uses multiple typefaces to establish clear visual hierarchy and guide readers through information logically and compellingly.

Challenge: Use typography alone (no images, minimal color) to organize and communicate information effectively.

Core Learning Goals

  • Information Hierarchy: Distinguish importance levels through typographic contrast (size, weight, color, spacing)
  • Typeface Pairing: Choose complementary fonts that work together
  • Visual Flow: Guide the reader's eye through information in intentional sequence
  • White Space Mastery: Use space strategically to organize information
  • Typographic Expression: Let font choices communicate mood and personality
  • Readability at Scale: Balance aesthetics with comfort and legibility

Understanding Visual Hierarchy

The Hierarchy Pyramid

Level 1: PRIMARY (Most Important)
Largest, boldest, most prominent

Level 2: SECONDARY (Important Supporting)
Medium size, supporting role

Level 3: TERTIARY (Details & Context)
Smallest, least visually prominent

Level 4: QUATERNARY (Fine Print)
Smallest, footnotes or disclaimers

The 70-20-10 Rule (Applied to Type)

  • 70% of visual attention → Primary element (headline, title)
  • 20% of visual attention → Secondary elements (subheading, supporting info)
  • 10% of visual attention → Tertiary elements (details, fine print)

Five Techniques for Creating Hierarchy (Without Color)

  1. SIZE: Primary 3-5x larger than body
  2. WEIGHT: Bold or extra-bold for primary, regular for body
  3. SCALE & PROPORTION: Dramatic size = dramatic importance
  4. ALIGNMENT & PLACEMENT: Center = formal, left = dynamic
  5. SPACING & DENSITY: Isolation = emphasis, tight = urgent

Typeface Pairing Guide

Successful Pairing Strategies

Strategy Example Pairing
Serif + Sans-Serif
(The Classic)		 Playfair Display (serif) + Open Sans (sans-serif)
Contrasting Sans Styles Montserrat (geometric) + Lato (humanist)
Heavy Display + Light Bebas Neue (heavy) + Garamond (refined)
Same Family, Different Weights Open Sans Bold + Open Sans Regular

Pairing Don'ts: ❌ Two serifs | ❌ Two display fonts | ❌ Too similar | ❌ Too different | ❌ More than 3 typefaces

The Design Process (12-17 hours total)

Step 1: Content Selection (1-2 hours)

Choose project type. Gather real content or create compelling fictional content. Understand information hierarchy.

Step 2: Typeface Research (1-2 hours)

Research 5-10 pairings. Make mood boards. Test fonts at actual sizes. Decide on primary and secondary fonts.

Step 3: Layout Sketching (2-3 hours)

Sketch 5+ directions by hand. Experiment with hierarchy. Test spacing and alignment. Don't get attached to first idea.

Step 4: Digital Mockup (4-6 hours)

Create document at proper size. Lay out information. Apply hierarchy principles. Adjust sizes, weights, spacing.

Step 5: Refinement & Testing (2-3 hours)

Print or view on large screen. Test hierarchy. Check legibility. Verify typefaces complement each other. Polish white space.

💡 Teacher's Tips: Testing Your Hierarchy

The "Five-Second Test"

Show layout for 5 seconds, then ask: Can they identify what's most important? Do they understand the primary message?

The "Squint Test"

Squint while looking: Does primary information stand out? Can you trace a visual path? Is white space balanced?

The "Print Test"

Print at full size and view from distance: Is text legible? Do typefaces look good together? Is spacing working?

Submission Format

  1. Digital Layout File: High-resolution PDF (300 DPI, proper dimensions)
  2. Source File: Original design file (.ai, .indd, .fig, or .afdesign)
  3. Typeface Pairing Rationale (250-350 words):
    • Why did you choose these typefaces?
    • How do they complement each other?
    • How did you create hierarchy without color?
    • Walk through your information organization decisions
    • How does typography communicate personality/purpose?
