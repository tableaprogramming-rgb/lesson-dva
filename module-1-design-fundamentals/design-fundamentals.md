### 1. Art vs. Design: The Fundamental Split
Before we design, we must define. Many students confuse creating "cool graphics" with design.

**Concepts Covered:**
- **Subjectivity vs. Objectivity:** Art is about how the creator feels (internal); Design is about how the user functions (external).
- **Expression vs. Problem Solving:** An artist paints to express a soul; a designer builds to solve a problem (e.g., "how can I make this button look clickable?").
- **Window vs. Door:** A famous analogy: Art is like a window (you look at it to see a new world); Design is like a door (you use it to get somewhere).

### 2. The Elements of Design (The Ingredients)
The Elements are the tangible "things" you use to build a design. If you can see it, itâ€™s made of these.

#### 2.1 Line
Lines are the path of a moving point. They create boundaries, lead the eye, and convey mood. 
- **Horizontal:** Stability and calm.
- **Vertical:** Strength and growth.
- **Diagonal:** Action and movement.

#### 2.2 Shape
When a line closes, it creates a shape. 
- **Geometric:** Structured, mathematical, and man-made.
- **Organic:** Fluid, unpredictable, and biological.

#### 2.3 Color
Color is the emotional engine of design. 
- **Hue:** The name of the color.
- **Saturation:** The intensity.
- **Value:** The light/dark property.

#### 2.4 Texture
The visual surface quality. Even on a flat screen, we can imply "roughness" or "smoothness" through patterns and grain.

#### 2.5 Space
The area around and within subjects. 
- **Positive Space:** The subject itself.
- **Negative Space:** The "White Space" that provides breathing room and focus.

#### 2.6 Form
Form is 3D shape. In 2D digital art, we create Form using shading and perspective to imply volume and mass.

#### 2.7 Value
The lightness or darkness of a color. Value is critical for creating depth and defining forms.

### 3. The Principles of Design (The Recipe)
Principles are the "rules" for how to arrange the Elements to communicate effectively.

#### 3.1 Balance
Visual weights should be distributed to create equilibrium. 
- **Symmetrical:** Formal and stable.
- **Asymmetrical:** Dynamic and modern.

#### 3.2 Contrast
The difference between two elements. Contrast is the "shouting" of a designâ€”the higher the contrast, the more attention it demands.

#### 3.3 Hierarchy
Ranking information by importance. A good designer tells the viewer what to look at 1st, 2nd, and 3rd.

#### 3.4 Rhythm & Movement
Repeating elements to create a visual "beat" that moves the eye across the canvas.

#### 3.5 Pattern
Strict repetition of elements to create structure and consistency.

#### 3.6 Emphasis
Creating a focal point. You can emphasize an object by making it a different color, larger size, or isolating it in negative space.

#### 3.7 Unity
The "Holy Grail" of design. Unity is the sense that all parts of the design belong together and work toward a single goal.

### 4. The Anatomy of the Self (Anatome)
The "Anatome" project is an exercise in visual identity. 

**Concepts Covered:**
- **Visual Representation:** Using lines, shapes, and textures to symbolize intangible traits like personality or ambition.
- **Self-Expression in Design:** How to translate personal history into a cohesive visual language.
- **Hierarchy of Interest:** Deciding what is the most important part of "you" to highlight visually.

---

### 5. The Power of Memory (Memento)
Design often relies on emotional resonance. The "Memento" project focuses on capturing nostalgia.

**Concepts Covered:**
- **Symbolism:** Choosing objects or colors that represent childhood or the past (e.g., a specific shade of yellow for a summer memory).
- **Color Psychology:** How different palettes evoke different emotional responses (warm colors for nostalgia, cool colors for clarity).
- **Metaphorical Thinking:** Using a visual "car" to represent "journey" or "moving forward".
